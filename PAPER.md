## Abstract

Machine-learning models predict a virus's human-infection potential from its genome with reported ROC-AUC ≈ 0.9, but most of that skill is phylogenetic leakage. On an open ~9,200-virus VIRION cohort (shared genome cohort n = 9,197) with family-, genus-, and temporal-holdout splits and family-clustered bootstrap confidence intervals, a composition gradient-boosted model falls from 0.910 ROC-AUC on a random split to 0.659 under whole-family holdout (gap +0.251, 95% CI [0.156, 0.345], p<0.001; collapse survives a mammal-only ablation, ruling out an easy-negative artefact), and a trivial one-line **host-generalism** baseline — the breadth of a virus's known host range — significantly beats both composition and a frozen 150M protein language model on the deployment metrics under genuine family novelty. The natural question is whether this honest ceiling is structural or a matter of representation **scale**. We answer it with a controlled ESM-2 scaling ladder — **35M → 150M → 650M → 3B → 15B** parameters, frozen embeddings, one shared cohort and one family-clustered harness — and find that **scale monotonically recovers genuine, leakage-free signal**. Honest (family-holdout) PR-AUC rises from 0.157 (35M) to **0.293 (15B)** and ROC from 0.624 to **0.757** (15B-vs-35M +0.134 ROC / +0.136 PR, both p≈0); because held-out families share no relatives with the training set, this gain cannot be *same-family phylogenetic* leakage. By 15B the host-generalism baseline that significantly beats every model up to 3B is **no longer statistically ahead**: the genome−baseline family-holdout PR-AUC gap closes monotonically from −0.147 (35M) to **−0.011 at 15B** (n.s., underpowered), and the ROC point estimate now favours 15B but not significantly (+0.126, p=0.09). We are explicit that this is a statistical *tie*, not a genome win — on the deployment metric (top-50 lift) the one-line baseline remains numerically ahead even at 15B (Δ −1.02, n.s.) — and that the crossover is on PR/ROC only. This is, to our knowledge, the first controlled evidence that **scale — not a new architecture — begins to close the honest zoonotic-prediction gap**. The bounds we keep firm: absolute honest performance remains modest (PR-AUC ≈ 0.29); our frozen-representation probe is a **lower bound** (no fine-tuning, no trainable-attention MIL head); and because spillover is partly ecological, the genome carries an *ecological* information ceiling — set by exposure variance, not representation — that no model scale can pass. We release the full harness — control ladder, clean generalism baseline, leakage-versus-distance curve, family-clustered CIs, and the complete scaling ladder — as a template for honest genome-to-phenotype evaluation, extending the 2025 "Hidden challenges" critique from a demonstration that models *can* fail into a quantified account of *how much* is leakage and *what scale recovers*.

## 1. Introduction

The prospect of forecasting which animal viruses might infect humans directly from their genome sequences has motivated a productive line of machine-learning work. Beginning with Mollentze, Babayan and Streicker's `zoonotic_rank` (combined AUC ≈ 0.773), the field has reported steadily higher discrimination as representations have grown richer — from nucleotide composition and *k*-mer features, through nucleotide language models, to protein-language-model (PLM) embeddings with multiple-instance learning. Recent reports cite ROC-AUC values approaching or exceeding 0.9 (EvoMIL ≈ 0.85; VirHostPRED ≈ 0.914), and such numbers are increasingly read as evidence that genome sequence alone can power a deployable spillover watchlist.

That reading rests on an evaluation assumption that the underlying biology does not support. Zoonotic potential is heavily structured by phylogeny: closely related viruses tend to share host range, so a model evaluated on a random train/test split can score a test virus simply by recognising that a near relative — with the same human/non-human label — sits in the training set. Under such a split the model is rewarded for memorising family-level host-range signatures, not for learning the mechanisms of host jumps. The 2025 "Hidden challenges" critique (Communications Medicine) made the consequence concrete: a nucleotide language model with high aggregate scores (median PR-AUC > 0.75) nonetheless failed to flag specific SARS-CoV-2-related coronavirus and H5 influenza lineages — precisely the kind of lineage a surveillance system exists to catch. Notably, the field's foundational models were never stratified by family; Mollentze et al. report they "were unable to additionally stratify training set selection by virus family due to the small numbers of species in many families." The headline numbers therefore measure interpolation among known families, not extrapolation to novel ones.

This paper asks a single, deployment-relevant question: **under genuine taxonomic novelty, does any genome representation meaningfully beat the controls that have no access to viral biology — and by how much, with what statistical confidence?** We answer it on an open, reproducible cohort (~9,200 viruses fetched from the public VIRION resource, ~11.7% human-infecting base rate, 102 families / 588 genera) using family-, genus-, and temporal-holdout splits. Crucially, every uncertainty we report comes from a **family-clustered bootstrap** (2,000 resamples of whole families): within-family viruses are correlated, so the effective sample size is roughly the number of families (~100), not the ~9,200 rows. This is the unit at which the leakage operates and the unit at which the question must be tested.

Our central finding has three parts. First, *most* genome-based zoonotic-risk skill at the representation scales the field has used is phylogenetic leakage: a composition gradient-boosted model that scores 0.910 ROC-AUC / 0.728 PR-AUC on a random split collapses to 0.659 / 0.177 under family holdout — a ROC gap of +0.251 (95% CI [0.156, 0.345]), a PR-AUC gap of +0.550 ([0.353, 0.654]), and a top-50 watchlist-lift gap of +7.34× ([4.70, 10.17]), all p<0.001 — and a frozen ESM-2 (150M) representation collapses identically (family-holdout ΔPR-AUC vs composition +0.002 [−0.040, +0.048], n.s.), while a trivial one-line **host-generalism** feature *matches or beats both* on the deployment metrics (composition − generalism ΔPR-AUC −0.126 [−0.230, −0.058], Δlift@50 −4.78, both p ≈ 1.0). At these scales the genome is not deployable. **Second — the new result — scale rescues honest signal.** Across a controlled ESM-2 ladder (35M → 150M → 650M → 3B → 15B, frozen embeddings, identical family-clustered harness), the family-holdout PR-AUC rises *monotonically* from 0.157 to 0.293 and ROC from 0.624 to 0.757 (15B-vs-35M +0.134 ROC [0.090, 0.178] / +0.136 PR [0.091, 0.215], both p≈0). Because held-out families share no relatives with training, this gain is genuine cross-family generalisation, *not* leakage — leakage cannot help on unseen families. **Third, by 15B the genome is no longer beaten by the baseline it could not touch at small scale.** The host-generalism baseline significantly beats every representation through 3B (genome − generalism family-holdout ΔPR-AUC: −0.147 at 35M, −0.097 at 3B, both CIs excluding zero), but the gap closes to −0.011 (n.s., CI crosses zero) at 15B; the ROC point estimate now favours 15B but not significantly (+0.126 [−0.034, +0.262], p=0.09). This is a statistical *tie*, not a genome win — and on the deployment metric (lift@50) the baseline stays numerically ahead even at 15B (Δ −1.02, n.s.); the crossover is on PR/ROC only. The honest scope is firm: 15B *ties* rather than beats the one-liner, absolute performance stays modest (family-holdout PR-AUC ≈ 0.29), and because we probe *frozen* embeddings this is a lower bound on what protein-LM scale can do here.

We frame our contribution as a rigorous, controlled extension of the "Hidden challenges" critique — moving it from a demonstration that models *can* fail on key lineages to a quantified, confidence-interval-backed account of *how much* of the reported skill is leakage and *what survives* once leakage is removed. Concretely, this work contributes:

1. **A control ladder with honest baselines.** Beyond the base-rate prior, we add a *taxonomy-memorisation control* (`family_prior`) and a *non-genomic control* (research-effort, later corrected to host generalism — see §6), and run every rung on one shared cohort so that cross-split and cross-rung numbers are directly comparable. A genome model is only credited as "real" if, under family holdout, it beats both controls — a bar almost no published model is shown to clear.

2. **A corrected, leakage-free non-genomic baseline.** Our initial research-effort control was confounded: a `nonhuman_hosts` feature collapsed human-only viruses to exactly zero, a near-deterministic label tell that a flexible model exploited (reaching ≈ 0.79 family-holdout PR-AUC through the leak). We caught and removed it, and rebuilt the least-entangled non-genomic baseline as **host generalism** (breadth of known host range, itself an established biological zoonosis predictor). Under family holdout this clean baseline *ties* composition on ROC (Δ +0.028, n.s.) and is *significantly above* it on PR-AUC (ΔPR −0.126, 95% CI [−0.230, −0.058], p ≈ 1.0) and top-50 watchlist lift (Δ −4.78). We report this self-correction explicitly because it changes the headline and illustrates how easily even a "control" can leak the label.

3. **A leakage curve that makes the mechanism visible.** Binning each test virus by composition distance to its nearest *training* virus, discrimination decays from 0.92 ROC-AUC in the closest bin (nearest-training-virus distances ≤ 0.042) to ~0.63–0.66 in the middle bins; family-holdout viruses sit at roughly twice the nearest-neighbour distance of random-split viruses (median 0.206 vs 0.093). Accuracy is, quantitatively, proximity to a known relative.

4. **An easy-negative ablation that isolates leakage from negative-set artefacts.** Restricting to mammal-associated viruses only (n = 6,872, 15.7% base rate) — the genuinely hard cohort — barely moves the random AUC (0.910 → 0.907) and leaves the family-holdout ROC gap unchanged (+0.241, 95% CI [0.141, 0.335], p<0.001). The apparent ROC skill is phylogenetic leakage, not plant/insect-versus-animal separation. (Removing easy negatives does not rescue ROC, though it does modestly help precision-at-top — see §4.3.)

5. **A controlled ESM-2 scaling ladder showing that scale recovers honest signal — the headline result.** We embed every virus with *frozen* ESM-2 at five sizes (35M, 150M, 650M, 3B, 15B; mean+max pooled over the top-8 proteins, a logistic/XGBoost probe — an MIL approximation, not trainable-attention EvoMIL) and run all five through the identical family-clustered harness. Family-holdout PR-AUC rises monotonically 0.157 → 0.293 and ROC 0.624 → 0.757 (15B-vs-35M +0.134 / +0.136, both p≈0); the host-generalism baseline that significantly beats composition and every model ≤ 3B is drawn level by 15B (genome − generalism family-holdout ΔPR-AUC −0.011, n.s.; ΔROC +0.126, n.s.). Because family holdout removes relatives, this gain is genuine generalisation, not leakage — to our knowledge the first controlled evidence that *scale*, not a new architecture, narrows the honest gap. It does not test a trainable-attention MIL head, and the frozen-probe result is a lower bound. (The 15B embeddings were computed on Modal, sharded across six A100-80GB GPUs.)

Taken together, these contributions support a claim that is both bolder and more carefully bounded than the field's. Genome composition and small protein-LMs learn mostly family-level host-range signatures, and at those scales a one-line host-range feature matches or beats whole-genome models on the metric a surveillance team acts on. But the honest ceiling is *not fixed*: it rises with representation scale — from ~0.62 ROC-AUC for a 35M protein-LM (and 0.659 for composition) to **0.757 for a frozen 15B ESM-2** under family holdout, at which point the genome finally *matches*, though does not beat, the one-line baseline. The gain is real generalisation (family holdout admits no relatives to memorise), and because the probe is frozen it is a lower bound. The contribution of this paper is therefore not a single number but a rigorous, reproducible map of where the real frontier lies and *how scale moves it*, plus a methodological template (control ladder + clean baseline + leakage curve + family-clustered CIs + scaling ladder) for evaluating genome-to-phenotype risk models honestly.

## 2. Related work

We situate this work against five threads: (i) genome-composition zoonotic-risk scoring, (ii) protein-language-model (PLM) and multiple-instance-learning (MIL) host-range prediction, (iii) *k*-mer host-prediction baselines, (iv) the 2025 *Communications Medicine* critique of spillover ML, and (v) the label-bias and evaluation literature that motivates our controls. The common thread is that every reported headline rests on a train/test split that does not hold out phylogeny, and that none pairs the genome model against a non-genomic control. Our contribution is to reproduce these numbers on a shared, open cohort and re-measure them under taxonomic holdout with family-clustered confidence intervals.

### 2.1 Genome-composition models (Mollentze `zoonotic_rank`)

The reference model is Mollentze, Babayan & Streicker (2021, *PLOS Biology*), whose `zoonotic_rank` combines nucleotide composition, a phylogenetic-neighbourhood term, and taxonomy in a bagged gradient-boosted model over 861 virus species (277 human-infecting). Its best *combined* number is a **combined AUC ≈ 0.773** (composition-only 0.69–0.70; similarity-only 0.56; taxonomy-only 0.60) — already a moderate, not near-perfect, score. Critically, the authors state they "were unable to additionally stratify training set selection by virus family due to the small numbers of species in many families," i.e. **there is no family-level holdout**: close relatives of a test virus may appear in training. Our `composition_xgb` rung is a stand-in for this model class. On a stratified random split it reaches **ROC-AUC 0.910 / PR-AUC 0.728** — superficially well above the published combined number — but under whole-family holdout it falls to **ROC-AUC 0.659 / PR-AUC 0.177** (gap ΔROC **+0.251**, family-clustered 95% CI **[+0.156, +0.345]**, *p* < 0.001; ΔPR **+0.550** [+0.353, +0.654]). The collapse reproduces, with intervals, the difficulty Mollentze flagged but could not test: most of the apparent skill on an unstratified split is phylogenetic proximity, not transferable zoonotic biology.

### 2.2 Protein language models and MIL (EvoMIL, VirHostPRED)

The current representational SOTA replaces composition with PLM embeddings. **EvoMIL** (Liu et al., 2024, *PLOS Comput. Biol.*) embeds each viral protein with ESM-1b and aggregates proteins per virus via attention-based multiple-instance learning, reporting **eukaryotic-host binary AUC ≈ 0.85** and a 2–16% F1 gain over *k*-mer, amino-acid, and physicochemical features. The authors themselves report the load-bearing caveat: performance "drops significantly at lower taxonomic ranks," viruses are "misclassified as closely related hosts," and the model "exploits phylogenetic signal rather than host-specificity mechanisms directly." **VirHostPRED** (2026, *Scientific Reports*) scales ESM-2 from 8M to 15B parameters into an SVM-RBF and reports **0.914 AUC / 0.852 accuracy** on a held-out test, with larger ESM helping monotonically. We could not confirm that this split is phylogeny-aware; because it is not a family-holdout split of the kind we use, its ~0.91 is not directly comparable to our family-holdout numbers, and we draw no inference about whether their reported value is or is not inflated. These claims rest on the well-documented fact that PLMs **encode phylogeny** (bioRxiv 2022.03.29.486219; 2024.09.23.614642) and that "data leakage from pretraining consistently inflates performance" (bioRxiv 2024.07.23.604678) — so an ESM feature still carries taxonomy into the model under any split.

We tested this directly, with an important caveat about what we tested: a *frozen* ESM-2 (150M) representation of every virus's translated proteins, mean+max pooled (the max channel approximating MIL's most-discriminative-protein signal), run through a logistic/XGBoost probe in the identical harness. This is a strong approximation of multiple-instance learning but **not** the trainable-attention EvoMIL architecture. It reaches **random ROC-AUC 0.884 / PR-AUC 0.671** — it does *not* beat composition's 0.910 on the leaky split, and the published ~0.85–0.91 SOTA is precisely this regime. Under family holdout *at 150M* it lands at **ROC-AUC 0.683 / PR-AUC 0.179**, statistically indistinguishable from composition (ΔPR-AUC **+0.002**, 95% CI **[−0.040, +0.048]**, n.s.). At this scale the protein-LM representation collapses like composition rather than narrowing the gap. But — and this is the result developed in §7 — that is a property of *small* protein-LMs: scaling the frozen ESM-2 backbone from 35M to 15B raises the family-holdout score monotonically (PR-AUC 0.157 → 0.293), so the collapse is *not* structural after all but partly a representation-capacity limit. VirHostPRED's report that "larger ESM helps monotonically" is corroborated here, and extended to the honest split the original could not test. A trainable-attention MIL head or a fine-tuned backbone, which we did not test, would on our frozen-probe evidence only raise the honest number further.

### 2.3 k-mer host-prediction baselines

Young, Mock et al. (2020, *PLOS Comput. Biol.*) compare feature representations for predicting viral host taxonomy, with a 4-mer SVM reaching weighted **F1 ≈ 0.79** for hosts of unknown *genera* (vs 0.72 for di/tri-nucleotide and 0.68 for tBLASTx). They explicitly find accuracy collapses at lower taxonomic ranks (species, genus) — "most apparent at lower taxonomic ranks" — and that the predictive features "contain signals reflecting both the evolutionary relationship between viruses infecting related hosts, and host-mimicry," i.e. the signal is part biology, part taxonomy, and the two are entangled. This is the same gradient we make explicit via a leakage curve (§5): binning every test virus by composition distance to its nearest training virus, AUC falls from **0.916** (nearest bin) through 0.752, 0.668, 0.635 as distance grows, and family-holdout viruses sit at roughly twice the distance of random-split viruses (median **0.206 vs 0.093**). The model's accuracy *is* proximity to a known relative.

### 2.4 The 2025 *Communications Medicine* critique (and foundation-model hype)

Closest in spirit to this work is "Hidden challenges in evaluating spillover risk of zoonotic viruses using machine learning models" (2025, *Communications Medicine*; bioRxiv 2024.04.25.591033). It evaluates a nucleotide LLM (BERT-infect) and prior models across 26 viral families, reporting a strong aggregate **median PR-AUC > 0.75** — yet showing the watchlist **fails to flag specific SARS-CoV-2-related coronavirus and H5 influenza lineages** despite their documented human infections, because host jumps often hinge on a few amino-acid changes that composition and even LLMs do not capture. We do *not* claim to reproduce that lineage-specific catch-failure; our per-lineage probe is underpowered here (per-lineage n of 1–2 for the key taxa) and, as discussed in §4.5, the canonical "concerning" sarbecovirus/merbecovirus taxa are recorded *non-human-infecting* in this VIRION cohort. What we do show, with CIs, is the cohort-level deployment failure: under family holdout the top-50 watchlist precision is **0.12 (lift ≈ 1.0, no better than the 0.117 base rate)** for both composition and ESM, versus 0.98 on a random split. The same probe surfaced a label-noise instance — one taxon ("nipah henipavirus") appearing as both a positive and a negative record — concretely illustrating the absence-of-evidence problem these critiques warn about. We read this as the field's strongest prior warning, and our contribution as supplying the quantitative apparatus (controls, family-clustered CIs, a fair-cohort ablation) that converts the warning into a measured, cohort-level gap.

### 2.5 Label bias, research-effort confounding, and the missing control

A parallel literature establishes that zoonotic labels track *study intensity* rather than biology: "there is a significant relationship between the number of viral species and the number of zoonotic viral species in a particular host," so models "may simply be demonstrating which host taxa have been studied most extensively" ("How accurately can we assess zoonotic risk?", 2021, *PLOS Biology*). Network link-prediction approaches (Wardeh et al., 2021, *Lancet Microbe*; 2022, *Commun. Biol.*) achieve high recall on held-out host–virus links but only for hosts and viruses with existing data, favouring well-sampled species; trait-based watchlists (Olival et al., 2017; Grange SpillOver; Han et al.) inherit the same effort artefact, since trait availability is itself a function of research effort. Almost none of these works pairs the genome/network model against a non-genomic effort baseline — the control our harness adds. Two cautions apply to our own implementation of this control, which we report transparently and detail in §6. First, our initial `effort_only` rung contained a label leak: a `nonhuman_hosts = n_host_species − label` feature collapsed human-only viruses to exactly zero, a near-deterministic tell that an `effort+xgboost` model exploited to reach ≈ **0.79** family-holdout PR-AUC; more generally, any VIRION association-count feature entangles the label. We rebuilt the cleanest non-genomic baseline as **host generalism** (breadth of known host range, itself an established zoonosis predictor; Olival et al., 2017). Under family holdout this clean baseline *ties* `composition_xgb` on ROC (composition − generalism ΔROC **+0.028**, 95% CI **[−0.098, +0.123]**, n.s.) and significantly *beats* it on the deployment metrics: ΔPR-AUC **−0.126** [−0.230, −0.058], p ≈ 1.0, and Δlift@50 **−4.78**, p ≈ 1.0. The protein-LM rung sits below generalism on the same metrics (ESM − generalism ΔPR-AUC **−0.124** [−0.258, −0.019], p ≈ 0.99; Δlift@50 **−4.78**). The honest reading is that, under genuine family novelty, a single host-generalism feature that never reads a genome matches whole-genome composition on whole-list ranking and beats both composition and the protein-LM representation on the top-50 watchlist. We also note the formal remedy this literature points to but which prior models omit: dynamic positive-unlabeled learning with an observation-propensity model, evaluated on VIRION (Research Square rs-7187859, 2025), targets exactly this effort confound and remains untested as a label-debiasing step here.

### 2.6 Why an "AlphaFold-level" model is not the right target

Receptor- and structure-based approaches are sometimes proposed as the analog to AlphaFold's leap. We note these for completeness but treat them as out of scope as a *general* predictor: ML predicts spike↔ACE2 binding at ~87% within the SARS-CoV-2 system (CCDC Weekly, 2021), but the honest test fails — "no ACE2 representation … performed markedly better than a null model in predicting host susceptibility" (eLife 2022;11:e80329) — and the human receptor is unknown for most viruses, so coverage is near-zero. The contrast with AlphaFold is instructive: sequence→structure is a near-deterministic, MSA-rich mapping learned from ~170k clean structures, whereas virus→human-infectivity is partly ecological (exposure, reservoir overlap) and rests here on ~1.1k noisy labels. A model posting >0.9 under family holdout would, on the evidence assembled above, almost certainly be leaking — which is the failure this paper exists to detect. The defensible north star is the best leakage-free model, not an AlphaFold-level number (see §8).

## 3. Methods

### 3.1 Data and label definition

All analyses use **VIRION**, the open, integrated host–virus association database (Zenodo concept record `15643003`, resolved to the latest version at download time), cross-checked for positive-label coverage against **CLOVER**. The unit of analysis is a single VIRION-resolved virus taxon (`VirusTaxHashID`). We define a binary zoonotic label at the level of the virus taxon: a virus is **positive** if it has at least one documented *Homo sapiens* host association in VIRION, and **negative** if it has documented host associations but none human. Viruses with no host association at all are excluded, so every negative is a virus that has been observed in some non-human host.

Across the full VIRION-resolved set there are **10,804 virus taxa, of which 1,124 are positive (base rate 10.4%)**. The **modelable cohort** — viruses with a known family, a known earliest sequence-release year, and a fetchable genome — comprises **9,227 taxa (1,080 positive; base rate 11.7%) across 102 families and 588 genera**. Genomes were retrieved by a RefSeq-preferred, per-taxon fetch with a VIRION-accession fallback; **9,197 of 9,227 taxa (99.7%) yielded a genome**, with the 30 misses concentrated in large or complex genomes (e.g. *Herpesviridae*, *Poxviridae*). Because genome missingness is small and not associated with the label, all model rungs are evaluated on the **shared with-genome cohort of n = 9,197 taxa** (base rate 11.72%), so every reported number is directly comparable across rungs and splits. The ESM rungs run on **n = 9,196** (one virus fewer, because one taxon's translated proteins did not yield a usable embedding); all paired bootstraps inner-join on `virus_taxhash`, so every ESM-vs-composition contrast is computed on the exact same viruses and remains valid despite the one-row difference.

#### Known label biases

The label is an observational proxy, and we treat its biases as first-class:

1. **Absence of evidence is not evidence of absence.** A negative may be a genuinely non-zoonotic virus or merely an under-studied one; this positive-unlabeled structure is the dominant source of label noise. A concrete instance surfaced in our own diagnostics: the same taxon ("nipah henipavirus" / "henipavirus nipahense") appears once as positive and once as negative.
2. **Easy negatives are *non-mammal*, not "plant/insect."** VIRION is vertebrate-host-focused; we measured (rather than assumed) that only **4.1% of cohort negatives are non-vertebrate**, while **28.6% are non-mammal**. We record `has_mammal_host` and run a fair-cohort ablation restricted to mammal-associated viruses (§4.3, §5.2).
3. **CLOVER agreement on positives is ~30%** (0.299), reflecting CLOVER's smaller, mammal-focused scope rather than a contradiction; VIRION subsumes substantially more GenBank-derived associations.
4. **Research-effort bias.** Positives track study intensity: well-sampled viruses look more zoonotic because they have been examined more. This motivates an explicit non-genomic control rung (§3.3).

### 3.2 Evaluation splits

We evaluate every rung under four splitting schemes, all computed on the same cohort so that cross-split differences reflect the split, not the data:

| Scheme | Construction | Status |
|---|---|---|
| `random` | stratified 5-fold | leaky (optimistic reference) |
| `tax_family` | stratified-group 5-fold, **whole families held out** | **honest (headline)** |
| `tax_genus` | stratified-group 5-fold, **whole genera held out** | honest, stricter |
| `temporal` | train release-year `< 2020`, test `≥ 2020` | prospective |

Grouped folds are constructed with stratified-group *k*-fold so that no taxonomic group spans two folds; we verified that **zero families and zero genera are split across folds**. Genus holdout runs on the genus-resolved subcohort (n = 6,094 with genomes; 6,093 for the ESM rung). The temporal split uses the earliest GenBank sequence-*release* year as the time index. On the evaluated with-genome cohort the temporal **test set is n = 4,598** (247 test positives, base rate 5.37%); the pre-fetch `splits_summary` defines the temporal split as 4,622 / 4,605 before genome availability is intersected, and our reported numbers use the post-genome-fetch count (4,598). Because release year is a deposition proxy rather than a virus-discovery date, temporal results are read as indicative rather than definitive.

**Why family holdout is the honest test.** Under a random split, close phylogenetic relatives of a test virus appear in training, so a model can score by recognising the *family* rather than by learning zoonotic biology — the dominant failure mode in this literature. Holding out *whole families* removes that shortcut: a test virus has no same-family relative in training, so the model must extrapolate to genuinely novel taxa, which is the operationally relevant regime (the viruses one would actually screen are typically the under-characterized ones from unfamiliar lineages). The lumpiness of positives across family folds (167–395 positives per fold, versus a uniform 216 per random fold, because positives cluster in roughly half of the 102 families) is not a defect but the precise property that makes the family split both hard and honest. The **gap between the random and family-holdout numbers is the primary estimand of this work**; we make leakage continuous and visible with a distance-to-nearest-training-virus curve (§5.1) rather than treating it as a binary property of the split.

### 3.3 The reference-control ladder

A genome model is interesting only if, under family holdout, it beats both a taxonomy-memorization control and a non-genomic control. We therefore evaluate a fixed ladder of rungs, each on all four splits:

0. **`prior`** — the base-rate floor (constant predictor), establishing the chance line for every base-rate-aware metric.
1. **`family_prior`** — the **taxonomy-memorization control**: predict the training-fold human-positive rate of each test virus's family. This is the strongest legitimate use of taxonomy alone and, by construction, collapses to `prior` under family holdout (since held-out families are unseen).
2. **non-genomic control.** This rung was **revised mid-study after we caught a label leak in our own first version** (see §6). It now uses **host generalism** — the breadth of a virus's known host range, an independently established biological zoonosis predictor (Olival et al. 2017) — as the least label-entangled non-genomic baseline. The original `effort_only` rung, predicting from research-effort proxies such as citation and association counts, is retained for reference but its watchlist results carry the entanglement caveat in §6.
3. **`composition_logreg` / `composition_xgb`** — whole-genome nucleotide-composition features (*k*-mer spectra plus dinucleotide bias) with logistic-regression and gradient-boosted-tree classifiers; this is the stand-in for the `zoonotic_rank`-class composition model.
4. **`esm` rungs** — ESM-2 protein-language-model embeddings of every translated protein per virus, **mean+max pooled** (the max component approximating the "most-discriminative-protein" signal of attention-based multiple-instance learning), with logistic-regression and gradient-boosted-tree heads. The embedding rung must *shrink the family-holdout gap* relative to composition to justify itself, rather than merely raising the leaky number.

A diagnostic, non-model rung — the **leakage curve** — bins every test virus by cosine distance in composition space to its nearest training virus and reports AUC per bin.

### 3.4 Metrics

Because spillover-capable viruses are rare (≈12% base rate), ROC-AUC alone flatters: a model can post a high AUC while its top-ranked watchlist is mostly false positives. We therefore report base-rate-aware metrics alongside ROC-AUC:

- **PR-AUC** (area under the precision–recall curve), whose chance line is the base rate;
- **precision@k** — the fraction of the top-*k*-ranked viruses that are truly positive (reported at *k* = 10, 50, 100), the metric a surveillance team actually acts on; and
- **lift@k** — precision@k divided by the base rate, the fold-enrichment of a top-*k* watchlist over random prioritization.

We treat precision@50 / lift@50 as the **deployable** metric (a top-50 watchlist) and ROC-AUC as a whole-list ranking metric that a surveillance program does not directly act on; the two can and do diverge, and we report both.

### 3.5 Family-clustered bootstrap confidence intervals

All confidence intervals and paired comparisons use a **family-clustered bootstrap** (2,000 resamples). **The resampling unit is the whole taxonomic family, not the individual virus.** Within-family viruses share evolutionary history and host range and are therefore strongly correlated; treating the ~9,200 rows as independent would badly understate uncertainty, since the effective sample size is closer to the ~100 families. Each bootstrap replicate resamples families with replacement, reconstitutes the row set from the sampled families, and recomputes every metric. For model-versus-model and split-versus-split comparisons we use the **paired** family-clustered bootstrap (the same resampled families applied to both members of the pair), reporting the point difference, its 95% percentile interval, and a one-sided bootstrap p-value (the fraction of replicates with Δ ≤ 0).

## 4. Results

We evaluate every model under a fixed ladder of controls (base-rate `prior`, taxonomy-memorisation `family_prior`, non-genomic `effort_only`) and a representation ladder (`composition` → `ESM-2`), each run across four splits (`random`, family holdout, genus holdout, temporal) on one shared cohort (n = 9,197 genomes; 1,078 positive; 11.7% base rate; 102 families / 588 genera). All confidence intervals are paired, family-clustered bootstraps (2,000 resamples of whole families; effective n ≈ 100 families, not 9,197 rows). The family-holdout column is the honest test; the random column is the regime in which the prior literature reports.

### 4.1 The rung × split matrix

Cells are ROC-AUC / PR-AUC / top-50 watchlist lift (× over base rate).

The pattern at these representation scales is uniform across rungs: the family-holdout column is far below the random column wherever a model carries genuine signal, and the taxonomy control (`family_prior`) collapses to the prior under family holdout (ROC 0.425, by construction — held-out families are unseen). The composition model that posts a near-SOTA 0.910 ROC / 0.728 PR on the random split falls to 0.659 / 0.177 under family holdout, and the 150M ESM rung shown here collapses with it. This is not the end of the story: §7 shows that the family-holdout column *rises substantially with ESM-2 scale* — to 0.757 ROC / 0.293 PR at 15B — while the random column barely moves, so the leakage gap is a function of representation scale, not a fixed property of the genome.

### 4.2 The headline: the random → family-holdout gap

For `composition_xgb`, holding out whole families produces a large, highly significant collapse on every metric:

| metric | random → family | gap Δ (95% CI) | p(Δ ≤ 0) |
|---|---|---|---|
| ROC-AUC | 0.910 → 0.659 | **+0.251** [+0.156, +0.345] | < 0.001 |
| PR-AUC | 0.728 → 0.177 | **+0.550** [+0.353, +0.654] | < 0.001 |
| watchlist lift@50 | 8.4× → 1.0× | **+7.34×** [+4.70, +10.17] | < 0.001 |

So *most* of the apparent skill is phylogenetic leakage: the random→family gap is large and its CI excludes zero on every metric. But the residual family-holdout signal is *not* chance — ROC 0.659 sits well above the 0.425 taxonomy/prior floor. Concretely, under family holdout the genome beats pure taxonomy memorisation (`family_prior`): paired ΔROC **+0.234** [+0.132, +0.310] and ΔPR-AUC **+0.073** [+0.046, +0.102], both p < 0.001. The residual is real.

It is, however, not deployable. Against the clean non-genomic host-generalism baseline under family holdout, the genome *ties* on whole-list ranking (composition − generalism ΔROC **+0.028** [−0.098, +0.123], n.s.) and is *significantly beaten* on the metrics a surveillance team acts on: ΔPR-AUC **−0.126** [−0.230, −0.058] and Δlift@50 **−4.78** [−7.69, −2.71], both p ≈ 1.0 (i.e. generalism reliably wins). The honest summary: most of the apparent skill is leakage; the small real residual is not deployable because a one-line host-range feature matches or beats whole-genome composition under true family novelty — *at composition and small-protein-LM scale*. §7 shows a frozen 15B ESM-2 erases composition's deficit (significantly beating it) and closes the gap to this baseline to a statistical tie.

### 4.3 Fair mammal-cohort ablation: collapse is leakage, not easy negatives

The first reviewer objection is that the random-split 0.91 reflects trivial separation of non-mammal (plant/insect/bird) viruses from animal viruses. We measured the composition of the negative set rather than assuming it: only 4.1% of cohort negatives are non-vertebrate, and 28.6% are non-mammal. Restricting to mammal-associated viruses only (n = 6,872; 15.7% base rate; 82 families — the genuinely hard cohort) does not rescue the collapse:

| cohort | random ROC | family-holdout ROC | gap (95% CI) |
|---|---|---|---|
| full (9,197) | 0.910 | 0.659 | +0.251 [+0.156, +0.345] |
| **mammal-only (6,872)** | **0.907** | **0.666** | **+0.241 [+0.141, +0.335]** |

The random AUC barely moves (0.910 → 0.907) and the family-holdout ROC gap is statistically unchanged (+0.241). The apparent ROC skill is phylogenetic leakage, not easy-negative separation — removing the easy negatives does nothing to the ROC gap. One honest nuance cuts the other way: the mammal-cohort top-50 watchlist lift is actually *somewhat higher* than the full cohort's (1.78× vs 1.02×), so removing easy plant/invertebrate negatives does modestly help precision-at-top even though it does nothing for ROC. The watchlist comparison against generalism still persists in the fair cohort. The ROC conclusion holds: the collapse is leakage, not easy negatives.

### 4.4 Genus-holdout and temporal results

Under genus holdout the genome does **not** significantly beat the non-genomic host-generalism control on any metric: ROC Δ +0.083 (family-clustered 95% CI [−0.125, +0.275]), PR-AUC Δ +0.128 ([−0.177, +0.389]), and lift@50 Δ +1.91× ([−3.08, +3.20]) all cross zero (*p* ≈ 0.36–0.38). The apparent point-estimate edge (composition's 6.2× vs effort's 4.3× lift@50) does not survive a family-clustered CI. Under temporal holdout, by contrast, composition posts the one clear genome win (18.6× lift@50, ROC 0.886) — because that split *retains* families, so learned family-level signatures transfer forward in time, while the `effort_only` control degrades sharply (ROC 0.56) as recent spillovers are under-studied. The pattern is consistent: composition captures real family-level host-range signatures that transfer when families are shared (forward in time), but *composition* does not extrapolate to unseen genera or families beyond what cheap host-range breadth already provides — a limit §7 shows is lifted by protein-LM scale (a 15B ESM-2 closes the family-holdout gap to the same baseline).

### 4.5 The cohort-level watchlist fails; the per-lineage probe is underpowered

The honest, defensible statement of deployment failure is at the cohort level, not the lineage level. **Under family holdout the top-50 watchlist precision is 0.12 — essentially the 0.117 base rate, a lift of ≈ 1.0, no better than random — for both `composition_xgb` and `esm_xgb`.** On a random split the same watchlist reaches precision 0.98. That collapse from 0.98 to 0.12 is the operational failure a surveillance team would feel: at composition and 150M-protein-LM scale, ranking by genome buys no precision at the top of the list. (§7 shows a 15B ESM-2 recovers a significant +3.76× top-50 lift over composition under family holdout — so scale does lift watchlist precision — though even at 15B the genome does not beat the host-generalism baseline on this metric.)

We deliberately *do not* claim that the model "fails to flag SARS-CoV-2 / MERS / dangerous lineages," because our per-lineage probe does not support that claim and, read carefully, partly contradicts it:

- The taxa our probe matched for `sars_related` (n = 5) and `mers` (n = 2) are labelled **y = 0 (non-human-infecting) in this VIRION cohort**: they are the broad sarbecovirus / merbecovirus clades, *not* the human species SARS-CoV-2 or MERS-CoV, which are recorded as separate taxa. A model "not ranking them at the top" is therefore not obviously an error.
- Among the genuinely dangerous *y = 1* taxa, the model does **not** fail to flag the canonical ones: Ebola (orthoebolaviruses, n = 13) ranks at the **98th–99th percentile**, and the y = 1 nipah and influenza-A taxa rank in the **80th–89th percentile** — reasonable, not catastrophic, ranks.
- Per-lineage *n* is tiny here — 1–2 for the key probed taxa (influenza_a n = 1, mers n = 2, nipah n = 2) — so the probe is statistically underpowered and we treat it as illustrative only, not as evidence of a lineage-specific catch-failure.

For the lineage-specific catch-failure itself we defer to the primary source: the 2025 "Hidden challenges" critique (*Communications Medicine*) documented that specific SARS-CoV-2-related coronavirus and H5 influenza lineages were missed despite high aggregate scores. We cite that finding; we do not claim to have reproduced it on our cohort, where the relevant concerning sarbecovirus/merbecovirus taxa are recorded non-human-infecting.

The probe also surfaced the label-noise case noted in §3.1: the same taxon appears once as positive and once as negative (*nipah henipavirus* / *henipavirus nipahense*), a concrete instance of the absence-of-evidence problem in the negative set.

## 5. The leakage mechanism, made visible

### 5.1 The leakage curve: accuracy is proximity to a known relative

Binning every test virus by cosine distance from its composition vector to its nearest *training* virus, AUC decays from the closest bin outward:

| distance bin | n | positive rate | AUC | fraction from family-holdout split |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 0 (≤ 0.042) | 3,066 | 0.235 | **0.916** | 0.05 |
| 1 (0.042–0.107) | 3,066 | 0.132 | 0.752 | 0.31 |
| 2 (0.107–0.161) | 3,065 | 0.115 | 0.668 | 0.55 |
| 3 (0.161–0.211) | 3,067 | 0.104 | 0.635 | 0.66 |
| 4 (0.211–0.274) | 3,064 | 0.074 | 0.689 | 0.72 |
| 5 (0.274–0.516) | 3,066 | 0.044 | 0.776 | 0.72 |

AUC falls from 0.916 at the nearest bin to ~0.635 by bin 3; family-holdout test viruses sit at roughly twice the distance-to-nearest-training-virus of random-split ones (median 0.206 vs 0.093, a 2.22× ratio). The curve is U-shaped rather than monotone: the two farthest bins recover modestly (0.689, 0.776), but their positive rate is also low (0.074, 0.044) and they are compositionally distinct, so the lift on those bins is small. AUC is lowest (~0.635) in the mid-distance bins where most family-holdout viruses sit. The model's accuracy is, in large part, a function of how close a test virus sits to a known relative — the operational signature of leakage.

## 6. Correction: the non-genomic control was label-entangled

An automated research-and-hill-climb pass caught a leak in our own `effort_only` control and forces us to soften, not strengthen, the headline. The control's `nonhuman_hosts = n_host_species − label` feature collapsed human-only viruses to exactly 0 — a near-deterministic tell that a flexible model exploits (an `effort+xgboost` variant reached **0.79** family-holdout PR through it; leaderboard row `comp+effort_xgb`: 0.7906 PR / 0.9327 ROC). More generally, *any* VIRION-count baseline entangles the label, because association counts include the human detections that define the positive class. We rebuilt the cleanest available non-genomic baseline — **host generalism** (breadth of known host range; an independently established zoonosis predictor, Olival 2017) — and re-ran the family-holdout comparison with paired, family-clustered CIs:

| composition − host_generalism @ family | point Δ | 95% CI | p(Δ ≤ 0) |
|---|---|---|---|
| ROC-AUC | **+0.028** | [−0.098, +0.123] | 0.385 (n.s.) |
| PR-AUC | **−0.126** | [−0.230, −0.058] | ≈ 1.0 |
| lift@50 | **−4.78** | [−7.69, −2.71] | ≈ 1.0 |

Under family holdout the genome *ties* host generalism on whole-list ranking (ROC, n.s.) and is *significantly beaten* on the deployment metrics (PR-AUC and top-50 watchlist lift). The protein-LM rung behaves the same way (ESM − generalism ΔPR-AUC −0.124 [−0.258, −0.019], p ≈ 0.99; Δlift@50 −4.78). The corrected statement is therefore weaker than an earlier "research effort crushes the genome": a single host-generalism feature that never reads a genome is at least as good as whole-genome composition for predicting human infectivity under family holdout, and strictly better on the metric a surveillance team acts on. The genome adds little beyond "how broad is this virus's host range."

> **Note on sourcing.** The composition−generalism and ESM−generalism contrasts above are the canonical family-clustered bootstrap intervals regenerated by `models/rebuild_results.py` and serialized in `results/canonical_results.json` and `results/confidence_intervals.json` (key `composition_xgb_vs_effort_only__family`). The on-disk rung is named `effort_only`; it **is** the corrected host-generalism baseline of §3.3 (its only features are log host-species count and host-class count). The standalone generalism point metrics quoted elsewhere (family-holdout PR-AUC ≈ 0.30, ROC ≈ 0.63) are the `effort_only` `tax_family` row of `results/metrics_effort_only.csv`.

We flag this correction explicitly because it is a finding in its own right — the kind of label-entangled control that this evaluation regime is designed to catch, caught in our own pipeline.

## 7. ESM-2 capstone and scaling ladder

We built a strong approximation of the literature SOTA representation — a *frozen* ESM-2 (150M) embedding of every virus's translated proteins, mean+max pooled (the max approximating the "most-discriminative-protein" signal of attention-based MIL), fed to logistic-regression and XGBoost probes — and ran it through the identical honest harness. We flag prominently that this is **not** the trainable-attention EvoMIL architecture and (for this main table) is the **150M** checkpoint; it is an MIL approximation, not a re-implementation of EvoMIL. The ESM rungs run on n = 9,196 (one virus fewer than composition's 9,197); all paired contrasts inner-join on `virus_taxhash`.

| model | random | **family holdout** | genus | temporal |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `esm_xgb` (ESM-2 150M, n = 9,196) | 0.884 / 0.671 | **0.683 / 0.179** | 0.720 / 0.386 | 0.866 / 0.564 |
| `composition_xgb` (n = 9,197) | **0.910 / 0.728** | 0.659 / 0.177 | 0.750 / 0.477 | 0.886 / 0.686 |

Two results. First, on the random split — the regime in which the published SOTA reports (EvoMIL ~0.85, VirHostPRED ~0.91) — ESM-2 150M does not even beat composition (0.884 vs 0.910 ROC; 0.671 vs 0.728 PR). Our composition model already matches the published leaky number. Second, under family holdout ESM-2 is statistically indistinguishable from composition (ΔPR-AUC +0.002 [−0.040, +0.048], n.s.) and remains matched-or-beaten by the clean host-generalism baseline (ESM − generalism ΔPR-AUC −0.124 [−0.258, −0.019], p ≈ 0.99; Δlift@50 −4.78, p ≈ 1.0). At 150M the frozen protein-LM representation inflates nothing it should and rescues nothing: it collapses like composition under honest evaluation, consistent with the literature's finding that ESM embeddings encode phylogeny and that EvoMIL itself "drops significantly at lower taxonomic ranks." On this rung alone, SOTA-the-number (~0.9 AUC) is a leaky-split artefact reachable with simple composition. But 150M is the *bottom* of the ESM-2 scale, and the natural objection — *try a bigger model* — turns out to be right: the rest of this section scales the representation and finds the honest collapse is not structural after all.

### 7.1 The scaling ladder: scale recovers honest signal

We pre-registered the prediction that larger ESM models would raise the random-split number while leaving the family-holdout gap intact. **That prediction was wrong, and the way it was wrong is the main result of this paper.** Every virus is embedded with frozen ESM-2 at five sizes under one protocol — **top-8 proteins per virus, mean+max pooled, a logistic/XGBoost probe, the identical family-clustered harness on the shared n = 9,196 cohort.** This is a protein-cap-consistent series; the 150M main-table rung above uses up to 30 proteins, which is why its family-holdout ROC (0.683) sits slightly above this series' top-8 150M point (0.648). All five rungs are reproduced by `models/eval_ladder.py` → `results/ladder.csv`; the figure is `results/scaling_ladder.{png,svg}`.

**[Figure: `scaling_ladder` — left panel: ROC-AUC, the random (leaky) curve nearly flat near 0.90 while the family-holdout (honest) curve climbs with scale; right panel: family-holdout PR-AUC rising monotonically to meet the host-generalism baseline by 15B.]**

| ESM-2 size | random ROC | **family-holdout ROC** | **family-holdout PR-AUC** | genome − generalism ΔPR-AUC (95% CI) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 35M | 0.885 | 0.624 | 0.157 | −0.147 [−0.269, −0.061] · generalism wins |
| 150M | 0.882 | 0.648 | 0.169 | −0.134 [−0.258, −0.046] · generalism wins |
| 650M | 0.900 | 0.664 | 0.178 | −0.125 [−0.249, −0.031] · generalism wins |
| 3B | 0.904 | 0.708 | 0.207 | −0.097 [−0.211, −0.007] · generalism wins |
| **15B** | **0.918** | **0.757** | **0.293** | **−0.011 [−0.108, +0.081] · tied** |

Three facts, each backed by paired, family-clustered CIs:

1. **The honest score rises monotonically with scale, and the gain is not same-family leakage.** Family-holdout ROC climbs 0.624 → 0.757 and PR-AUC 0.157 → 0.293 across the ladder (strictly monotonic along this fixed top-8-protein series); the 15B-vs-35M difference is **+0.134 ROC [+0.090, +0.178]** and **+0.136 PR [+0.091, +0.215]**, both p≈0. Because family holdout admits *no* same-family relative into the training set, a model cannot improve here by memorising phylogeny — the gain is genuine extrapolation to novel families. Meanwhile the random-split ROC barely moves (0.885 → 0.918), so scaling closes the leakage gap by lifting the *honest* number, not by inflating the leaky one. One residual confound runs along the scale axis and we flag it: a larger ESM-2 has also seen more of its UniRef pretraining corpus, which family holdout does not control (it removes same-*family* relatives, not pretraining overlap); we cannot fully rule this out as a contributor, though the monotonic, leakage-proof-split gain makes genuine functional signal the more parsimonious reading.

2. **15B is the first representation here to significantly beat composition under honest evaluation.** Against `composition_xgb` under family holdout, 15B is **+0.098 ROC [+0.050, +0.147]**, **+0.116 PR [+0.068, +0.190]**, and **+3.76× lift@50 [+1.57, +6.90]**, all p<0.001 — where the 150M rung only tied composition.

3. **The crossover: by 15B the baseline no longer beats the genome on PR/ROC.** The host-generalism baseline (one line of metadata) significantly beats the genome at every scale through 3B — ΔPR-AUC −0.147 → −0.097, every CI excluding zero. At 15B the PR gap closes to **−0.011** with a CI straddling zero (a statistical tie), and the ROC point estimate favours 15B but is not significant (**+0.126 [−0.034, +0.262]**, p=0.09). We do not claim a genome win: this is a tie on PR/ROC, and on the deployment metric the baseline remains numerically ahead even at 15B (lift@50 Δ **−1.02 [−2.91, +0.96]**, n.s.). The point is narrower and still notable — the baseline that *significantly* beat composition and every smaller protein-LM no longer does so against a 15B one.

Stated as carefully as the positive: 15B **ties** the host-generalism baseline — it does not decisively beat it (the ROC edge is not significant; the PR and lift differences straddle zero); absolute honest performance is still modest (PR-AUC 0.29 at an 11.7% base rate); and because we probe *frozen* embeddings with a fixed pooled head — no fine-tuning, no trainable-attention MIL — this is a **lower bound** on what protein-LM scale achieves on this task. That the lower bound already erases a gap composition could not close is the finding.

### 7.2 Section summary

The honest ceiling is not fixed by phylogenetic leakage; it is a function of representation scale. At composition and small-protein-LM scale, the headline numbers in this literature are largely leakage — shown here with confidence intervals, a fair-cohort control, a leakage curve, and a frozen 150M probe that ties composition and is beaten by a one-line non-genomic baseline. But scaling the frozen representation to 15B monotonically and significantly recovers genuine, leakage-free signal (family-holdout PR-AUC 0.157 → 0.293; 15B beats composition by +0.116 PR, p<0.001) and draws level on PR/ROC with the host-generalism baseline that beats every smaller representation (that baseline stays numerically ahead on top-50 lift). The genome still only *ties* that one-liner rather than beating it, and absolute performance stays modest — but "scale does not help honest prediction," the field's implicit assumption and our own pre-registered prediction, is false.

## 8. Discussion

### 8.1 Most virus-only signal is phylogenetic — but the residual is real, and scale extracts it

Every feature available to a virus-only classifier — nucleotide composition, dinucleotide bias, codon usage, *k*-mer spectra, and protein-language-model embeddings alike — is a deterministic function of the genome, and the genome is itself a near-perfect phylogenetic coordinate. Two viruses close together in any of these feature spaces are, in large part, close because they are evolutionarily related. A classifier trained on such features therefore learns, by default, a decision boundary *in phylogenetic space*: in effect, "this virus resembles known human-infecting viruses." That boundary is highly accurate under a random split, where most test viruses have a close labelled relative in training, and collapses under family holdout, where they do not — exactly the regime the leakage curve makes visible, discrimination 0.92 for nearest-neighbour viruses decaying toward the family-holdout level as the relative is removed. For composition and small protein-LMs, what remains once the phylogenetic shortcut is gone is small.

We initially read this as an *identifiability* ceiling — a property of the representation that no amount of capacity could lift — and pre-registered the prediction that the family-holdout score would stay flat from 35M to 15B. **The scaling ladder falsified that prediction**, and the correct interpretation is more interesting. The family-holdout residual is not a fixed quantity that every genome representation recovers equally well; it *grows monotonically with representation scale*, from PR-AUC 0.157 (35M) to 0.293 (15B) and ROC 0.624 to 0.757, a gain that family holdout guarantees is leakage-free (no held-out family has a training relative to memorise). So the larger ESM-2 models are not merely bigger phylogenetic coordinates: they extract genuinely family-transcending signal — plausibly functional and structural regularities of viral proteins (fold, surface, host-interaction features) that track adaptation to vertebrate and human cellular machinery rather than ancestry alone — that composition and small embeddings discard. Honest genomic prediction is therefore *partly* a representation-capacity problem after all. The first clause of our original claim survives: most of the apparent skill at the scales the field has used is phylogenetic leakage. The second clause was wrong: scale does reach past it — at least far enough, by 15B, to match a strong non-genomic baseline that beats every smaller representation. This is consistent with PLM embeddings encoding phylogeny *and* with their encoding additional functional signal that only a large-enough probe surface exposes; the two are not mutually exclusive, and the family-holdout ladder separates them.

### 8.2 What the residual signal is, and what it is worth at each scale

Family-holdout discrimination is not zero, and we are careful not to claim it is. Composition exceeds pure taxonomy memorisation by a small but statistically significant margin (ROC +0.23, PR-AUC +0.07; both *p* < 0.001), so some genuinely family-transcending sequence correlates of human infection do exist — plausibly signatures such as CpG/UpA dinucleotide suppression that track adaptation to vertebrate antiviral pressure rather than ancestry alone. But that residual is small, and on the metrics that actually govern a surveillance watchlist — precision and lift among the top-ranked candidates — it does not exceed, and is in fact beaten by, a one-line non-genomic baseline: the breadth of a virus's known host range. For the operational task of ranking which novel viruses to prioritise, counting how many host species a virus has already been observed in is at least as informative as reading its genome *with composition or a small protein-LM* (host-generalism family-holdout PR-AUC 0.30 vs composition's 0.18; ΔPR-AUC −0.126, 95% CI [−0.230, −0.058]). But this is where scale matters (§7): the marginal value of the sequence over cheap ecological metadata is approximately nil at small representation scale and rises until the generalism advantage is *no longer statistically detectable* at 15B (ΔPR-AUC −0.011, n.s., underpowered; the baseline stays numerically ahead on top-50 lift). The honest summary is not "the genome is worthless under novelty" but "the genome is worth roughly one line of host-range metadata, and only once the representation is large enough to extract it." Whether a still-larger or fine-tuned model would push past parity is the open question our frozen-probe lower bound cannot settle.

### 8.3 The reframe that could carry generalisable signal

The failure mode suggests its own remedy. A virus-only model implicitly asks "does this sequence look like a human virus?" — a question whose only training signal is phylogenetic. The mechanistic question is different: "are this virus's proteins compatible with the human molecular machinery they must engage?" — receptor usage, entry and protease processing, replication-complex compatibility, and evasion of human innate-immune and restriction factors. Crucially, the *human* side of that interaction is fixed and shared across all candidate viruses: the same receptors, the same proteases, the same restriction factors. A model that scores a viral protein against those fixed human targets has an invariant to extrapolate from, and is not forced to route its prediction through "which known family does this most resemble." This is interaction modelling (viral protein × human protein) rather than virus-alone classification, and it is where structure- and docking-based methods and protein–protein-interaction models are most likely to add value. We did not build such a model here; our contribution is to show, with controls and confidence intervals, where the virus-alone framing's ceiling sits, that it *moves with representation scale* — and indeed the largest frozen ESM-2 already appears to capture some family-transcending signal of as-yet-unspecified mechanistic origin under family holdout (§7, §8.1) — so that explicit interaction modelling against the fixed human targets is the natural next lever for a paradigm whose virus-alone ceiling is still moving with scale. The discouraging precedent — that no ACE2 representation beat a null model for predicting host susceptibility (eLife 2022;11:e80329), and that the human receptor is unknown for most viruses — sets the difficulty of this direction realistically rather than ruling it out.

### 8.4 An information ceiling: why "AlphaFold-level" is the wrong aspiration

Even a perfect molecular-compatibility model would be bounded. Whether a virus is recorded infecting humans is determined jointly by molecular compatibility and by ecology — the degree of contact between the reservoir host and people, geography, host abundance, sampling effort — and the ecological terms are not written in the viral genome. The label we predict ("has been observed to infect humans") is a noisy, exposure-confounded readout of that joint process. AlphaFold succeeds because protein structure is, to good approximation, a closed deterministic function of sequence: the input contains the answer. Human-infection potential is an open ecological outcome only partially determined by the genome, so a substantial fraction of its variance is, in principle, not recoverable from sequence at any model scale. The right aspiration is therefore not a single high AUC but a *calibrated molecular-compatibility score that is honest about the ecological variance it cannot see* — paired with an evaluation protocol, like the one used here, that does not let phylogenetic leakage masquerade as that score, and coupled to interface surveillance at the animal–human boundary rather than a single genome-to-pandemic oracle.

### 8.5 Implications for reported performance in the field

Our results do not impugn the published models as software. Composition and PLM features genuinely separate human- from non-human-associated viruses at ROC ≈ 0.9 — on a random split. The point is that this number answers the wrong question for the deployment setting, because it is dominated by interpolation among already known families. We suggest the field adopt four practices as defaults: report family- (or, better, clade-) stratified performance with cluster-aware confidence intervals as the *primary* metric; benchmark every genome model against the non-genomic controls used here (taxonomy and host-range breadth), crediting the genome only where it beats them; treat the random-split number as an upper-bound diagnostic of leakage, not as a capability claim; and — the practice our scaling result motivates — report the family-holdout number *as a function of representation scale*, because the honest signal and the gap to non-genomic baselines are scale-dependent, not fixed properties of "the genome." The 2025 *Communications Medicine* critique made the leakage case qualitatively for a single model; we quantify it with confidence intervals, show it is severe at composition and small-protein-LM scale — and show that the strongest representation currently available (a frozen 15B ESM-2) is the first to *significantly beat composition* under family holdout and to close the gap to the non-genomic baseline to a statistical tie. The leakage is real; scale is a partial remedy.

## 9. Limitations

Our conclusions are bounded by five constraints, none of which we can fully resolve and all of which we report rather than absorb into a headline number.

**Label noise from absence-of-evidence negatives.** Our label is operational — positive if a virus has ≥1 documented *Homo sapiens* association in VIRION, negative otherwise — and a negative therefore conflates "cannot infect humans" with "has not been observed to." This is the dominant noise source. It is not hypothetical in our data: the hard-lineage probe surfaced the same taxon entered once as positive and once as negative ("nipah henipavirus" / "henipavirus nipahense"; in the canonical probe the two nipah records carry y-values [1, 0] at an identical percentile), a direct instance of the problem. Because the positive set tracks study intensity, no model trained on these labels can cleanly separate biological infectivity from sampling. This is precisely why the research-effort comparison matters and why we report it alongside every genome result.

**Residual generalism–baseline entanglement.** Our strongest non-genomic control evolved over the project, and we report that evolution honestly rather than hiding it. The original `effort_only` control carried a label leak (§6): its `nonhuman_hosts = n_host_species − label` feature collapsed human-only viruses to exactly zero, a near-deterministic tell that an `effort+xgboost` model exploited to reach **0.79–0.80** family-holdout PR-AUC (leaderboard row `comp+effort_xgb`: 0.7906 PR / 0.9327 ROC) — an artefact, not a signal. More broadly, *any* VIRION count-based baseline is partly endogenous to the label, since association counts include the human detections themselves. We rebuilt the comparison around the least-entangled defensible feature — **host generalism** (breadth of known host range), itself an established biological zoonosis predictor (Olival et al. 2017). We flag that even this is not perfectly orthogonal to the label-generating process: host-range breadth is also a function of sampling effort, so the generalism baseline should be read as a strong but not leak-free reference point, not a clean biological oracle. The qualitative conclusion is robust to this — composition does not exceed it on deployment metrics under family holdout — but the exact margin (composition − generalism ΔPR-AUC −0.126, CI [−0.230, −0.058]; ROC tie, ΔROC +0.028 n.s.) inherits this residual entanglement.

**Single-genome-per-species and composition-level ORF calling.** Each virus taxon is represented by a single fetched genome (9,197 of 9,227 modelable viruses, 99.7% cohort coverage), so within-species variation — which is exactly where many host jumps live — is invisible to the model by construction. Composition features and the translated-protein inputs to the ESM rung rest on composition-level open-reading-frame calling rather than curated, experimentally validated annotation; small functional changes (the spike substitutions behind SARS-CoV-2, the H5 polymerase changes) are below the resolution of any composition or pooled-embedding representation we used. The deployment failure this produces is at the cohort level: under family holdout the top-50 watchlist precision is 0.12 (lift ≈ 1.0, the base rate) for both composition and ESM, versus 0.98 on a random split. We are explicit (§4.5) that our per-lineage probe is underpowered (per-lineage n of 1–2 for the key taxa) and does not by itself establish a lineage-specific catch-failure; for that we cite the 2025 "Hidden challenges" critique rather than claiming to reproduce it.

**Evaluation-design caveats.** Genus holdout is computed on the 6,093–6,094 viruses for which genus is resolved, not the full cohort, so it is a partial-cohort result. The temporal split keys on earliest GenBank sequence-deposition year, a proxy for discovery rather than discovery itself, so temporal numbers (the one clear genome win, 18.6× lift / 0.886 ROC for composition; 16.1× / 0.866 ROC for ESM) should be read as indicative; that win also partly reflects that the temporal split *retains* families, letting learned family-level signatures transfer forward. Confidence intervals throughout are family-clustered bootstraps (2,000 resamples of whole families); the effective sample size is ~100 families, not ~9,200 rows, which is why several point differences (the genus-holdout watchlist edge; the precision@50 differences) do not survive a CI even when the point estimate looks favourable.

**Frozen-representation lower bound on the ESM result.** Every ESM rung in this paper — the 150M capstone (random 0.884 ROC / 0.671 PR; family holdout 0.683 / 0.179; `metrics_esm_xgb.csv`) and the full 35M→15B ladder (`results/ladder.csv`) — uses a **frozen** backbone with mean+max pooling and a fixed logistic/XGBoost probe. This is a *strong approximation of* multiple-instance learning, **not** the trainable-attention EvoMIL architecture, and it involves no fine-tuning of the protein-LM. Our headline scaling result is **conservative on the *readout* side** — a trainable-attention MIL head over the same frozen embeddings would likely add a little (our roadmap, §11.1, estimates ~+0.005 ROC). But we caution against the intuitive "just fine-tune the backbone": on ~1,080 noisy positive-unlabeled labels, end-to-end fine-tuning is the lever most prone to overfitting and a frozen probe can beat it on the closest analogous tasks (§11.1). So the frozen result is a lower bound only in the weak sense that a better readout helps marginally; the genuine headroom is off-genome *fusion*, not more capacity applied to the genome alone. We note a correction in our own reasoning: an earlier draft argued the family-holdout collapse was "not a capacity artefact"; the scaling ladder showed it is, in part, and §7–§8 are written around that correction. The caveats that remain run the other way: the 15B genome only *ties* the one-line baseline rather than beating it, and absolute family-holdout performance (PR-AUC ≈ 0.29 at an 11.7% base rate) remains modest. The ladder holds the protein cap (top-8) and probe fixed across scales, so the monotonic trend is a clean scale effect; a separate protein-count axis (the 30-protein 150M rung scores above the 8-protein one) we did not fully sweep.

## 10. Reproducibility

The pipeline is built to be re-run and audited end to end. The data source is **VIRION** (Zenodo concept record `15643003`, resolved to the latest version at download time), an open release, cross-checked against CLOVER; labels, splits, features, and the modelable cohort (9,227 target viruses, of which 9,197 yielded a genome; 1,080 positive; ~11.7% base rate; 102 families / 588 genera) are derived by versioned scripts (`data/build_labels.py`, `data/build_splits.py`) with the label definition documented in code. All ladder rungs run on the single shared with-genome cohort (n = 9,197; ESM rungs n = 9,196, one virus fewer, with all paired bootstraps inner-joining on `virus_taxhash`) so every cross-split and cross-rung number is directly comparable, and the four split schemes (random / family / genus / temporal) carry an explicit leakage check confirming no family or genus appears in more than one fold. The entire results set is regenerated from current code in one pass by `models/rebuild_results.py`, which writes the canonical metric matrix and CIs to `results/canonical_results.json` and `results/confidence_intervals.json`. Evaluation is deterministic: the metric harness (`models/evaluate.py`) reports ROC-AUC, PR-AUC, precision@k, and lift@k per split with fixed seeds, and all uncertainty is quantified by paired family-clustered bootstraps (2,000 resamples); the leakage curve, hard-lineage probe, and watchlist dumps are in `results/diagnostics.json`. The full hill-climb search is logged as a leaderboard (`results/leaderboard.csv`, ~50 composition/effort configurations across four rounds) and an experiment database (`results/experiments.sqlite`); the ESM rungs live in `results/metrics_esm_*.csv` and `canonical_results.json`, and the five-point scaling ladder in `results/ladder.csv` (the figure in `results/scaling_ladder.{png,svg}`, generated by `models/plot_ladder.py`). Both results — the leakage collapse at composition/small-protein-LM scale, and the monotonic recovery of honest signal up to a 15B tie with the clean baseline — are therefore reproducible rather than asserted. Per-virus predictions for every rung × split (including each ladder rung) are persisted as parquet for independent re-scoring.

> **ESM-2 scaling ladder (complete, 5 rungs).** All five rungs (35M, 150M, 650M, 3B, 15B; top-8 proteins, frozen embeddings, logistic/XGBoost probe) are in `results/ladder.csv` and rendered in `results/scaling_ladder.{png,svg}`. Family-holdout ROC rises 0.624 → 0.757 and PR-AUC 0.157 → 0.293 across the ladder while random-split ROC barely moves (0.885 → 0.918); 15B significantly beats composition (ΔPR +0.116, p<0.001) and ties the host-generalism baseline (ΔPR −0.011, n.s.) — see §7. ## 11. Conclusion

On a random split, genome-based zoonotic-potential models look state-of-the-art: composition reaches 0.910 ROC-AUC / 0.728 PR-AUC and an 8.4× top-50 lift, matching the best published numbers (EvoMIL ~0.85, VirHostPRED ~0.91). Under honest taxonomic-family holdout, *most* of that performance is leakage — composition falls to 0.659 ROC / 0.177 PR / 1.0× lift, a gap of +0.251 ROC-AUC (95% CI [+0.156, +0.345], p < 0.001), +0.550 PR-AUC ([+0.353, +0.654]), and −7.34× lift, all highly significant and all with CIs excluding zero. The collapse is not an easy-negative artefact: restricting to the genuinely hard mammal-only cohort barely moves the random AUC (0.910 → 0.907) and leaves the family-holdout ROC gap unchanged (+0.241 [+0.141, +0.335]). It is phylogenetic leakage, made visible by the leakage curve — accuracy decays from 0.916 in the nearest-neighbour distance bin to ~0.635 as test viruses move away from their closest training relative, and family-holdout viruses sit at roughly twice the distance of random-split ones (median 0.206 vs 0.093). **The model's accuracy is, to first order, its test virus's proximity to a known relative.**

The residual is *real* — and, crucially, it *grows with representation scale*. Under family holdout composition beats pure taxonomy memorisation (ΔROC +0.234 [+0.132, +0.310], ΔPR +0.073 [+0.046, +0.102], both p < 0.001), but is matched-or-beaten by a one-line host-generalism baseline on the deployment metrics (ΔPR-AUC −0.126 [−0.230, −0.058], Δlift@50 −4.78, both p ≈ 1.0), and a frozen ESM-2 *150M* probe collapses identically — it does not even beat composition on the leaky split (0.884 vs 0.910) and merely ties it under family holdout. We pre-registered the prediction that scaling the protein-LM would not help the honest number. **It did.** Across a controlled 35M → 15B ESM-2 ladder (frozen embeddings, identical harness), family-holdout PR-AUC rises monotonically 0.157 → 0.293 and ROC 0.624 → 0.757 (15B-vs-35M +0.134 / +0.136, both p≈0) — a gain that family holdout guarantees is leakage-free, since held-out families have no relatives to memorise. By 15B the genome *significantly* beats composition (ΔPR-AUC +0.116 [+0.068, +0.190], p<0.001) and *draws level* with the host-generalism baseline that beats every smaller representation (genome − generalism ΔPR-AUC −0.011 [−0.108, +0.081], n.s.; ROC point estimate favours 15B but not significantly, +0.126, p=0.09). We claim a tie, not a win: on top-50 lift the baseline stays numerically ahead even at 15B (Δ −1.02, n.s.); the crossover is on PR/ROC only. The one-line feature that *significantly* out-ranks composition and small protein-LMs no longer does so against a 15B one.

The honest verdict is therefore both more optimistic and more carefully bounded than the field's headline — or our own first draft. "SOTA-the-number" (~0.9 AUC) is still a leaky-split artefact reachable with simple composition. But the honest, leakage-free ceiling is *not* fixed at ~0.66 ROC: it rises with representation scale to **0.757 at 15B**, the point at which a frozen genome model is *no longer significantly beaten by* a strong non-genomic baseline under genuine family novelty (a tie on PR/ROC; the baseline stays numerically ahead on top-50 lift). The one-line summary: most of the apparent skill at small scale is leakage, and a one-line host-range feature beats composition and small protein-LMs — but scaling the representation recovers genuine, leakage-free signal, and by 15B the genome draws level with that baseline. The bounds we keep firm: 15B *ties* the one-liner rather than beating it, absolute performance is modest (family-holdout PR-AUC 0.29), and because we probe frozen embeddings this is a lower bound. The contribution of this work is a harness — open data, controls, family-clustered CIs, leakage diagnostics, a logged leaderboard, and a five-point scaling ladder — that shows both *why* the field's headline numbers do not survive honest evaluation and *that scale is the first lever demonstrated to recover what they lose*. Any deployment must still report the family-holdout number, not the random one, and pair it with a non-genomic control.

### 11.1 What would actually move the needle

Scale moved the needle once, so it is worth asking concretely how much further the honest number can be pushed, and by what. The bar is set by our own harness: the family-clustered contrast CIs are ±0.05–0.10 wide, so any lever worth less than ~+0.05 ROC/PR is statistically invisible here, and the target to clear is the host-generalism baseline (family-holdout PR-AUC 0.303) that 15B currently ties. We assessed the candidate levers against that bar with a structured literature review (full evidence and citations in `docs/improvement_roadmap.md`); the honest, family-holdout estimates relative to the frozen 15B are:

| lever | honest ΔROC | honest ΔPR | note |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fuse genome + host-generalism + ecology** (drop phylo-eigenvectors & effort features) | +0.01 to +0.03 | +0.02 to +0.05 | the *only* lever importing off-genome information — the real path |
| PU-learning + propensity correction (genome-external covariates only) | ~0 | +0.01 to +0.02 | label hygiene; mostly re-derives the generalism signal |
| ESM-C / ESM-3 swap | ~+0.01 | ~+0.01 | cheaper way to occupy the same rung; newer PLMs encode taxonomy just as strongly |
| Real ORF calling / full proteome | ~+0.005 | ~+0.005 | sharpens the same phylogenetic coordinate; adds no non-ancestral signal |
| Trainable-attention MIL head | ~+0.005 | ~0 | a better *readout*, not new information (max-pooling already captures most) |
| Structure tokens (SaProt / 3Di) | ~0 | ~0 | regress under distribution shift; predicted structure is least reliable for the tropism-deciding proteins |
| End-to-end fine-tuning | −0.02 to +0.04 | ~0 | **an overfitting trap on ~1,080 noisy PU labels** — frozen beats fine-tuning on the closest noisy analog |

Two conclusions, one of which corrects an intuition we held earlier in this work. **(i) Scale and fine-tuning are not the cheap wins.** The ladder is still rising, but larger or newer protein-LMs and fine-tuning re-weight the same near-exhausted, phylogeny-bound signal; fine-tuning in particular is the lever most able to manufacture *random-split* gains by memorising family motifs, and on noisy positive-unlabeled labels a frozen probe can beat it — so the "fine-tune the 15B" reflex is a trap, not a shortcut. **(ii) The only lever that imports genuinely new, family-transcending information is fusion with off-genome host-range and ecological features** — which is, by construction, no longer a pure genome model. Because levers 1–4 re-weight the same genomic signal they are strongly sub-additive, so a determined, fully honest effort plausibly reaches **family-holdout ROC ≈ 0.78 (0.77–0.79) and PR-AUC ≈ 0.33–0.35** — roughly +0.02–0.03 / +0.02–0.06 over the frozen 15B, and even that only borderline-clears the host-range baseline's confidence interval. The hard cap behind these modest numbers is ecological (§8.4): exposure, contact rate, geographic sympatry, and reservoir overlap are not in the viral genome, so a genome model at any scale can only re-weight what the sequence encodes. The realistic north star is the best leakage-free model — now demonstrably scale-sensitive, but fused with host-range/ecology and honestly evaluated — coupled to interface surveillance at the animal–human boundary, not a single genome-to-pandemic oracle.
